NASA Summer School Topic 3: Variability of Clouds and Precipitation

Goal: To examine the spatial and temporal variabilities of clouds and precipitation and evaluate how climate models simulate them.

Project Description

Topic: Variability of Clouds and Precipitation

Motivation:

  • Clouds have compensating cooling and warming radiative effects and whether cloud effects increase or decrease with global warming is uncertain. Cloud feedback is one of the primary contributors to climate sensitivity.
  • Precipitation is vital to life on Earth and latent heating associated with precipitation drives global atmospheric circulation; however, climate simulations of precipitation and its change have large discrepancies.
  • Variabilities of clouds and precipitation are closely coupled.

Datasets:

  • MODIS cloud fraction (including liquid and ice cloud fraction)
  • GPCP/TRMM/GPM precipitation
  • AMSR-E sea surface temperature (and optional ECMWF-Interim)
  • CERES longwave and shortwave radiative fluxes
  • CMIP5 historical simulations of cloud fraction, precipitation, and radiative fluxes

Geographic foci: global, tropics (15S-15N), subtropics (15-30S/N), mid-latitude (30-60S/N) and selected regions (ITCZ, northeast Pacific and southeast Pacific, etc)

Questions:

  1. What are the spatial distributions of clouds, cloud radiative effects and precipitation? (2-D maps; zonal-mean plots)
  2. How are clouds and precipitation distributions related to large-scale circulation, SST and other quantities (PW, RH, LTS, CAPE etc)? (correlation maps, scatter plots, conditions sampling)
  3. What are the seasonal and interannual variations of clouds, cloud radiative effects and precipitation? Are there detectable trends? (time series, EOF analysis)
  4. How do climate models simulate the spatial and temporal variabilities of clouds and precipitation?
  5. What are the “emergent constraints” for climate sensitivity and hydrological sensitivity?
